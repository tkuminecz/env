# Belief Burden Catalog

## What Is a Belief Burden?

A belief burden is the specific cognitive weight preventing clear thinking about a problem. The dialectic frees the user from their belief burden by having the skill carry it — the user operates from a belief-free position above both positions, analyzing structure rather than defending territory.

**Identifying the burden:** During the Phase 1 interview, pay attention to:
- What the user finds hardest to say against their preferred position
- Where they hedge when asked to steelman the opposing view
- What assumption they treat as axiomatic rather than contingent

**Do not announce your diagnosis.** Use these patterns internally to calibrate the thesis/antithesis and what each position must hold with full conviction.

## Catalog of Common Belief Burdens

### The Premature Converger
**Pattern:** Has already locked onto an answer and is looking for confirmation, not exploration. Can describe the opposing view, but only in its weakest form.

**Signals:** "I already know what I think, I just want to stress-test it." Describes opposing view briefly, with dismissive framing. Ends sentences with "...but obviously that doesn't work because..."

**What the dialectic must do:** The Thesis validates the core insight of their preferred position (freeing them from having to defend it). The Antithesis must be at *full conviction* — not a critique of their position but a genuinely alternate view that doesn't engage with their framing at all. The goal: force them to encounter a position that doesn't feel like a response to theirs.

**Calibration note:** These users are often the most resistant to synthesis. The synthesis must not feel like a compromise toward the position they dismissed. It must feel like it came from somewhere outside both.

---

### The Paralyzed Balancer
**Pattern:** Can see merit in every position. Cannot commit because every commitment feels like a betrayal of what the other side got right. Produces exhaustive lists of considerations.

**Signals:** "On one hand... but on the other hand..." repeated. Cannot answer "what would you do right now?" without extensive qualification. Lists pros and cons at roughly equal length.

**What the dialectic must do:** Both Thesis and Antithesis must be *fully committed and mutually exclusive* — no hedging, no acknowledgment of the other side. The goal is to force the paralyzed balancer to experience two fully-committed positions simultaneously, which is what their own cognition prevents. The synthesis must cut to a *decision criterion*, not just a synthesis of considerations.

**Calibration note:** The synthesis for this pattern often takes the form: "The decision criterion is X. Under X, [Thesis/Antithesis] is clearly correct. If you can't commit to X as the criterion, that's the real question."

---

### The Values Conflicted
**Pattern:** Holds multiple commitments that conflict with each other. Each commitment is individually correct; collectively they're impossible. Feels like a crisis of integrity.

**Signals:** Describes multiple things they care about, each in strong moral terms. Gets stuck because choosing feels like betraying a value, not just a preference. Asks questions like "how do I reconcile these?"

**What the dialectic must do:** Thesis and Antithesis each take *one of the user's own values* and push it to its logical extreme. The contradiction comes from within the user's own value system — not from external critics. The synthesis must operate at the level of *priority criteria under conditions of conflict*, not at the level of which value is correct.

**Calibration note:** These users often need the synthesis to give them permission to prioritize — not to dissolve the values conflict but to provide a principled basis for triage.

---

### The Experienced Skeptic
**Pattern:** Has seen many approaches fail. Evidence of past failures is used as a prior strong enough to prevent engaging with new arguments. Cites "in my experience" frequently.

**Signals:** Dismisses arguments with specific counterexamples from past experience. Very high burden of proof for new positions. Describes why current approach works and why alternatives have failed before.

**What the dialectic must do:** Thesis validates the current approach — *specifically* why the experience base supports it (honors their evidence, not just their conclusion). Antithesis must argue from *different evidence* — not abstract principles but concrete cases where the current approach has historically failed, preferably in adjacent domains. The goal: create encounter with evidence the experience base doesn't contain.

**Calibration note:** Synthesis for this pattern often takes temporal or conditional form: "Your experience base is accurate for [conditions X, Y, Z]. The Antithesis evidence is from [conditions A, B, C]. The synthesis is: [how to identify which set of conditions you're in]."

---

### The Principle Defender
**Pattern:** Has invested identity in a particular position or approach. Challenges feel personal. Engages more with the challenger's credibility than with their arguments.

**Signals:** Responds to arguments by questioning source or motivation. Becomes more committed when challenged (backfire effect). Framing is often moral ("the right way to do X").

**What the dialectic must do:** Thesis must honor the genuine insight behind their position — *exactly* the insight they'd want honored. The Antithesis must argue from entirely different principles, not engage with or rebut the Thesis's principles. The goal: make the Antithesis feel orthogonal, not threatening. Synthesis must preserve their core insight explicitly.

**Calibration note:** State explicitly in the synthesis what is preserved from their position before stating what is cancelled. Order matters — defended identities open when the preservation is acknowledged first.

---

### The Novel Explorer
**Pattern:** Loves exploring new ideas. Gets excited by new frameworks and connections. Has difficulty settling on a conclusion because each new input reopens the question.

**Signals:** Introduces new angles frequently. Asks "but what about...?" before a question is resolved. Describes the problem space as richer than anyone has appreciated. Has explored this before without resolution.

**What the dialectic must do:** Thesis and Antithesis must be *complete* — not leaving hooks for new angles to enter. The goal is not more exploration but *disciplined closure*. The synthesis must include an explicit *commitment rule*: "Here is the signal that says you know enough. Here is when further exploration is diminishing returns."

**Calibration note:** For this pattern, the synthesis is often less about the object-level question and more about the meta-question: "What would it look like to actually decide this? What's the decision criterion you're avoiding?"

---

## Using This Catalog

1. During Phase 1, identify the dominant pattern from the interview signals.
2. Calibrate Thesis and Antithesis prompts (or your own framing) based on what each position *must hold* for maximum cognitive leverage.
3. Calibrate the synthesis format based on what the user needs most: a decision criterion, permission to prioritize, preservation of their core insight, or a commitment rule.
4. Do not announce the pattern name to the user.
