# Strategic Decision Domain Calibration

## When the Domain Is Strategy or Product

Strategic decisions differ from technical ones in important ways:

1. **The objective function is contested.** Technical debates usually share a success metric. Strategic debates often don't. The first move is almost always: establish what the shared goal *actually is*, because both sides may be optimizing for different things.

2. **Market and customer evidence is privileged.** Abstract strategic principles ("focus beats breadth") are far less valuable than specific customer or market evidence. Both thesis and antithesis should be grounded in real examples, not theoretical arguments.

3. **Time horizon is usually the hidden variable.** "Build vs. buy," "grow vs. profit," "platform vs. feature" — almost all strategic tensions dissolve when time horizon is specified. The synthesis frequently involves making time horizon explicit and conditional.

4. **Optionality is often the real value.** The synthesis frequently identifies that one path preserves more future optionality, which is the real argument — not which is "better" in the abstract.

## Common Shared Assumptions to Interrogate

**Market structure assumption:** "The market works like [X]." Is the market actually winner-take-most, or does it support multiple players? Both sides often assume the same market structure when this is actually the contested question.

**Capital assumption:** "Given our current runway/budget..." Both sides often treat capital constraints as fixed. Does the synthesis change if capital constraints are different?

**Competitive assumption:** "Competitor X is/isn't a threat." Both positions often share a model of competitive dynamics that may be wrong.

**Customer definition assumption:** "Our customer is [X]." Both positions often share an implicit customer definition. The synthesis sometimes lies in: "for customer segment A, [Thesis]. For customer segment B, [Antithesis]."

**Timing assumption:** "Now is the time to [X]." Is the timing claim actually justified, or is it an inherited assumption?

## Strategic Synthesis Patterns

**The Staging Synthesis:**
Both positions are correct at different stages of company or product maturity. The synthesis specifies the stage transition criteria.

Example: "Grow now, optimize unit economics at $X ARR/when [specific signal]."

**The Portfolio Synthesis:**
Both positions are correct for different bets. The synthesis is a portfolio allocation: "Pursue [A] for the core business; run [B] as a bounded experiment with explicit success criteria."

**The Optionality Synthesis:**
The synthesis identifies which path preserves more future options. "Both approaches get us to [goal]. [A] preserves the option to [X]. [B] preserves the option to [Y]. Which option is more valuable?"

**The Sequential Synthesis:**
One position creates the conditions for the other. "Do [A] to build [capability/position]. From that position, [B] becomes viable in a way it isn't today."

**The Segmentation Synthesis:**
The thesis is correct for segment X; the antithesis is correct for segment Y. Synthesis: explicit segmentation strategy.

## Common Anti-Patterns in Strategic Dialectics

**Benchmarking disguised as reasoning:** "Company X does [A], therefore [A]." The correct response to benchmarking is: "What were Company X's specific conditions, and do those conditions apply here?"

**Narrative consistency over truth:** Both positions have been repeated so many times internally that they feel like facts. The dialectic must return to *first-principles evidence*.

**Urgency inflation:** "We must decide X by [date]." Real urgency is often manufactured. Is the deadline actually binding? What's the cost of deciding in 30 days instead of today?
